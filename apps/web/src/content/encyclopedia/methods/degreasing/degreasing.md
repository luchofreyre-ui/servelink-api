## What This Is

Degreasing is a cleaning method designed to break down oily, fatty, and sticky soils that do not respond well to neutral cleaners alone. It is commonly used on kitchen cabinets, backsplashes, range hoods, appliances, and floors near cooking areas.

## Why It Happens

Grease soils are hydrophobic, meaning they resist plain water. Cooking aerosols, food oils, hand transfer, and heat exposure create films that bind to surfaces and can polymerize over time, especially near stoves and ovens.

## What People Do Wrong

Most people either under-clean grease with neutral spray cleaners or over-attack it with abrasive scrubbing. They also skip dwell time, which is essential for breaking oily films down before agitation.

## Professional Method

Assess the surface first. Select a degreaser appropriate for the finish and soil level. Apply evenly, allow dwell time, agitate with a non-scratch towel or brush, rinse or wipe residue away, then dry to prevent streaks and re-deposition.

## Data and Benchmarks

Grease removal typically improves when chemistry is matched to soil load and temperature. Light grease may release in a single pass, while layered or polymerized buildup often requires repeat application and controlled agitation.

## Professional Insights

Heat and cooking vapors create some of the most deceptive residue in a home because the surface may look dusty while actually carrying an oily film. Effective degreasing relies on chemistry and dwell time far more than brute scrubbing.

## When to Call a Professional

Call a professional when grease has layered for long periods, when cabinets have delicate finishes, when residue has spread to ceilings and walls, or when commercial-grade buildup is present.
